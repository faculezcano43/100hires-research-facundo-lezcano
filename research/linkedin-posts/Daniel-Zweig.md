POST NUMBER 3
Yesterday I had the pleasure of speaking about AI enablement in success workflows at ICSEF- The Israeli Customer Success Executives Forum.
Everyone talks about AI at a high level - big visions, strategy slides, many buzzwords. BUT yesterday I focused on what really matters - the down-to-earth, actionable, TACHLES part:
- How to move from pilot AI chaos to AI that’s part of the daily work.
- How to turn a GenAI idea into a working workflow - with simple, easy-to-execute steps.
- And how we turned WiseStamp to became part of the 5% of companies whose AI initiatives actually go live.
The MAIN message from yesterday?
1. Focus on simplified, modular processes - Break your workflows into small, manageable parts, and address each part with a tailored AI solution. Start with replacing real concrete tasks and workflows, don’t make up new ones (yet). And mostly manage expectations around AI’s actual abilities. Don't try to start with the most complex task in your company, or your fantasies - start with tasks that are easy to digest.
2. Minimum time - gradual value - Like a new employee hire think of AI agents as a gradual process. First, don’t get into long exhausting ones, and spend minimum time on pilot to get results quicker. Build fast, optimize later.
3. Self-reliant stack - No code based - Build a self-dependent department, that can move fast, make decisions easily and is not reliant on other parts of the company (IT or R&D) (and of course use Zapier or N8N for quick enablement).
4. Ownership and optimization process - The "clients" of the Agents output should be the owners of it - give them access. Don't be afraid of sharing. They define the goals, they understand the needs and they will do the QA. Embed accessible QA and monitoring workflows (Just use Google Sheets for God's sake). Oh and - Prompt engineering is a skill everyone in your company should know.
5. Build the AI muscle in the company - AI isn’t another project. It’s a muscle. You build it gradually, one small workflow at a time. Start with keeping it simple and move fast. Don’t chase perfect - first learn how to use it, and every workflow you build will make the next one easier and smarter. Oh and please please please - don’t measure only based on immediate ROI. The ROI lies within the process itself.
I always enjoy speaking in public - sharing knowledge, getting challenged by questions, and hearing honest feedbacks - But the best feedback I got this time? Was "how practical and down to earth yesterday's lecture was."
Because I honestly thing that today (mostly here) too many talk about complex AI strategies and the "amazing things they do with AI", while very few can show real outcomes (and sometimes even real understanding) - In my perspective - it’s not about saying big words, it’s about doing.
